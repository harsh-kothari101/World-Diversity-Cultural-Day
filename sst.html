  • Here you will get to know more about sacred rivers.

  • Water is a source of life, energy, and absolution in religions and communities around the world. Temples, cities, and even empires have been built around water, and from Christianity to Hinduism to the Nigerian Yoruba religion, rivers have always played an especially important role in worshippers lives. Unfortunately today, many of these important rivers have been polluted and sacred sites destroyed by industrialization and urban waste. Yet despite the destruction, many modern pilgrims still travel to them to find guidance, mercy, or a final resting place.

  • Here, five sacred rivers—some revered by hundreds, others by millions—around the world :


    • Columbia River



    A short note on Columbia River

    Much of what made the U.S.'s Columbia River sacred has been decimated
    by local Oregon state and federal governments, which built three dams
    in the Columbia River Gorge. The dams all but killed off the salmon
    migration through the area and flooded sacred fishing areas, like Celilo
    Falls, for the Nez Perce, Umatilla, Warm Springs, and Yakama nations.

    Jordan River



    A short note on Jordan River

    The Jordan River connects the Dead Sea with the Mediterranean,
    passing through Israel, Jordan, Lebanon, and Syria. It's sacred in both
    Judaism and Christianity for different reasons: In the Old Testament,
    ancient Jews had to cross the river as a final step in finding freedom
    after years wandering the desert; in the New Testament, Jesus
    christens John the Baptist in the waters. The exact spot of John's
    baptism is best pinpointed to Al-Maghtas, Jordan, now a UNESCO
    World Heritage site     , and followers of both faiths can enter
    the murky river here, though it is heavily polluted.


    Osun River



    A short note on Osun River

    Nigeria's Yoruba religion has influenced polytheistic religions like Santería,
    practiced in much of Latin America, and Candomblé, in Brazil, after the
    Yoruba people were transported across the globe during the slave trade.
    The religion is based on 401 spirits, or orishas, and the Osun River is connected
    to Yoruba's Oshun, the goddess of water and love.
    A festival in a UNESCO-protected grove in Osogbo, near the river's
    start, celebrates the goddess and the river attributed to her each August.

    Urubamba River



    A short note on Urubamba River

    Cutting through Peru's lush Sacred Valley, the Incans saw the Urubamba as a direct
    reflection of the Milky Way, a celestial river known as " Mayu." Flowing along the
    spiritual center of Incan culture, the river passes important ruins like Machu Picchu
    and the Raqch'i temple. According to Incan beliefs, the river was a collection
    point of the Earth's water, that would then feed into the sky.


    Whanganui River



    A short note on Urubamba River

    New Zealand’s Whanganui River has long been sacred to the local Māori,
    who view it as a living, breathing ancestor, and last year it became the
    first river in the world to be granted the same legal rights as a human being.
    The Māori had been petitioning for recognition of their sacred relationship
    with the 180-mile long river since the 1870s, and the Kiwi government
    has invested $78 million (U.S.) in reparations, forming a legal framework
    for the river's personhood, and maintaining its well-being.
